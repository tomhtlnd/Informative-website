FOSSIL FUELS = GREENHOUSE GASES = A BURNING PLANET.

The energy sector is the largest contributor to global greenhouse gas emissions that are wariming the planet, primarily due to burning fossil fuels. It accounts for over 75 percent of global greenhouse gas emissions and nearly 90 percent of all carbon dioxide emissions.

Fossil fuels have dominated the energy mix for over a century, but their environmental impacts cannot be ignored any longer. We need to end our reliance on fossil fuels and invest in alternative sources of energy that are clean, accessible, affordable, sustainable, and reliable.

The good news is that renewable energy technologies, such as wind and solar power, are already available and in many cases, cheaper than fossil fuels. By accelerating the transition to clean energy, we can mitigate the effects of climate change and safeguard the planet for future generations.

With the clock ticking, it is more important than ever that we accelerate the transition to clean energy and hold governments and corporations accountable for their actions. At the same time, we must educate ourselves and each other about the importance of this issue and take individual actions to reduce our carbon footprints. So, how are we doing? Let's explore the data and find out.

Current state

This section provides an overview of the energy transition and explores some of the key challenges and opportunities associated.

Global energy generation

Despite increasing global attention on the need to transition to cleaner energy sources, this graph shows that fossil fuels still account for the vast majority of global energy production.

In fact, non-renewable energy sources, including coal, oil, and gas, still account for over 70 percent of the world's total energy supply, while renewable energy sources such as solar, wind, and hydropower account for less than 30 percent.

This highlights the urgent need to accelerate the transition towards renewable energy sources and reduce our reliance on fossil fuels.

The worlds capacity

The world's energy infrastructure is still heavily reliant on fossil fuels, which continue to dominate the global energy mix. While the capacity of wind and solar has increased significantly in recent years, the current energy infrastructure is simply not capable of supporting the production of more sustainable energy on a large scale.

This highlights the need for urgent investment in the development of renewable energy technologies and infrastructure. Without significant progress in this area, it will be difficult to achieve the necessary reductions in greenhouse gas emissions to mitigate the worst impacts of climate change.

Fossil fuel dependence: China's challenge for sustainable energy

China is the largest population center in the world, with both high energy demand and a high polluting consumption profile. The country relies on imported energy, most of which comes from fossil fuels. Although China is investing in and expanding renewable energy, it remains reliant on emissions-intensive fuels.

The United State's domestic energy production

The United States has a highly industrialized economy and is one of the largest energy consumers in the world. They have abundant domestic production of energy with significant amounts of fossil fuels and nuclear power and are likely to remain an energy exporter. In recent years, the country has been increasing its renewable energy capacity, with a particular emphasis on wind and solar energy.

Powering a Nation: India's Energy Challenge

India is one of the fastest-growing economies in the world, with a large and growing population that is driving an increase in energy demand. Like China, India relies heavily on imported fossil fuels to meet its energy needs, although the country is working to expand its renewable energy capacity.

Fueling the World: Russia's Energy Dominance

Russia is one of the world's largest energy producers and has large reserves of oil, natural gas and coal. These non-renewable energy sources form the backbone of the energy sector and an essential source of revenue. Renewable energy capacity remains a relatively small share of the country's total energy production, and is hardly expected to increase.

Fossil fuel subsidies

Find out about fossil fuel subsidies and their role in the energy transition

What are fossil fuel subsidies?

Fossil fuel subsidies are financial incentives given by governments to reduce the cost of production and consumption of fossil fuels such as coal, oil, and gas. These subsidies are one of the most significant barriers to the global shift to renewable energy sources, hampering efforts to achieve net-zero carbon emissions.

Although politicians have repeatedly pledged to end this type of support, fossil fuel subsidies continue to persist. According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), 52 advanced and emerging economies gave subsidies worth an average of $555 billion annually from 2017 to 2019.

These subsidies often take the form of tax breaks, direct payments, or artificially low fuel prices. There are two types of subsidies: production subsidies, which are common in Western countries, and consumption subsidies, which are more prevalent in lower-income countries.

The main challenge in ending fossil fuel subsidies is the discrepancy between the rhetoric and the reality, as some governments continue to regard them as helping citizens to benefit from a country's endowment of natural resources.

Renewable energy

Explore the rise and trends of renewable energy sources

Renewable energy trends

In the past twenty years, the installation of renewable energy sources has increased significantly, with particular focus on hydropower, wind, and solar energy. One of the most important factors has been the reduced cost of manufacturing solar panels. As a result, solar energy is becoming more affordable and accessible to the general population. This trend shows a growing shift towards renewable energy sources as an alternative to non-renewable energy sources like fossil fuels.

However, despite this progress, renewable energy still only accounts for a relatively small percentage of the total energy mix, and more needs to be done to accelerate the transition away from fossil fuels. In addition, renewable energy sources have their own set of challenges, particularly in terms of infrastructure and construction. For instance, hydropower plants have been known to cause significant ecological harm, while the installation of solar panels can pose serious threats to air quality.

Overall, while the trend towards renewable energy is positive, more action is needed to address the challenges and accelerate the transition towards a sustainable energy future.

Hydropower

Hydropower harnesses the energy of water moving from higher to lower elevations. It can be generated from reservoirs and rivers.

Wind

Though average wind speeds vary considerably by location, the world’s technical potential for wind energy exceeds global electricity production.

Solar

The rate at which solar energy is intercepted by the Earth is about 10,000 times greater than the rate at which humankind consumes energy.
